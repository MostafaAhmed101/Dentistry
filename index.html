History of Dentistry

Dentistry is considered one of the oldest branches of medicine that has evolved over the ages, dating back to ancient civilizations that began with primitive attempts to treat dental pain using simple means. Over time, this field has witnessed remarkable developments, transforming from folk remedies into a comprehensive science based on advanced medical principles. Today, dentistry is regarded as one of the leading medical fields that combines prevention and treatment, providing people with healthy and beautiful smiles that reflect humanity's long-standing concern for oral health.

EARLIER TIMES: PERIOD OF ANTIQUITY

During these early periods, not many divisions existed in the art of healing. Ailments pertaining to teeth and jaws were treated in a general manner, not requiring the services of a specialist. Hippocrates , the Father of Medicine, was the first to give a scientific basis to understanding and treating diseases, before which anomalies were mainly treated by sacerdotal medicine. The cause of diseases was often attributed to superstitions requiring sacrifices and rituals to appease a supernatural power. One of the earliest cases was found in the Sumerian text, which describes 'tooth worms' as the cause of tooth decay. The Indus Valley Civilization has yielded evidence of dentistry being practiced as far back as 7000 BC. A site in Mehrgarh indicates that this earliest form of dentistry involved curing tooth-related disorders with bow-drills operated, perhaps, by skilled bead craftsmen. The earliest dental filling, made of beeswax, was discovered in Slovenia and dates from 6500 years ago. It is logical that progress in dentistry would go hand in hand with the stability of a particular civilization, and the art of dentistry flourished in one of the oldest civilizations, the Egyptians.

DENTISTRY IN CHINESE HISTORY

The Chinese called the toothache 'ya-tongy' and classified toothaches according to their cause. They developed around 18 decoctions for toothache in the form of pills and mouthwashes. Along with these pills, they also used acupuncture as a means to treat toothache and gum diseases with about 26 puncture points for toothache and six for gum problems; some of these points were located at parts of the body distant from the oral cavity. They also used aromatic powdered leaves in a process called 'moxibustion' to relieve tooth pain.

DENTISTRY IN EGYPTIAN HISTORY

Here, as in other countries, medicine and dentistry were practiced mainly by the sacerdotal caste, but not all the people in the caste were priests and doctors at the same time. There was a special class of people known as the 'Phostophori' whose mission was to cure the sick. As evidenced by the tomb of Hesy Re, who is often regarded as the first dentist, he stated, "the greatest of those who deal with teeth, and of physicians." This is the first known reference of a person being identified as a dental practitioner. The Egyptian text known as Ebers Papyrus talks about various ailments of the oral cavity and remedies for the same, including treatment for dental abscess, remedies to strengthen gums for swelling, and tooth pain. There were also attempts at replacing lost teeth, as discovered by archaeologists, with teeth united by a gold wire, some of which were from another individual.

THE BEGINNINGS OF A PROFESSION: THE MIDDLE AGES

The Arabians

The Arabians certainly had the merit of keeping alive the study of medicine in an age of decadence and barbarism. Rhazes, or more precisely, Abu Bekr Muhammed ben Zacarjaer Rhazi, stressed the need to exhaust every method to cure teeth before extracting them, hence propagating conservation of tooth structure. To prevent the spread of caries, cavities were filled with a cement made of mastic and alum, which is a laudable attempt at stopping the progress of tooth decay. Against periodontitis and the pains produced by it, he recommended, in addition to opium, oil of roses, pepper, and honey, as well as the scarification of the gums and the application of a leech. If, however, these remedies did not succeed, he applied castoreum, pepper, ginger, storax, opium, and other ingredients to the roots of the teeth. If even this method of cure failed, he touched the root of the diseased tooth with a red-hot iron or sought to provoke its fall by the use of special medicaments such as coloquintida and arsenic.

((Ali Abbas)), in the eighth century AD, a Persian physician, wrote about diseases of the tooth and explained the treatment of a periapical lesion by introducing hot needles into the tooth repeatedly until they became cool. He also used chemicals in the tooth like asafetida to devitalize the tooth and relieve the pain. If these methods failed, he would extract the tooth.

Serapion ((Jahiak Ebn Serapion)), who lived in the tenth century, indicated with great precision the number of dental roots. Against dental pains of phlogistic origin, he recommended bloodletting, purgatives, and many local medicaments. In cases of persistent odontalgia due to caries, he advised the application of opium in the carious cavity. To strengthen loosened teeth, he first employed astringents, and if these were of no use, he bound the loose tooth to the neighboring healthy ones by means of gold or silver wire.

One of the greatest luminaries of medicine among the ((Arabs)) was Ebn Sina. He was born in 980 AD and recommended drilling a tooth with constant pain to remove humors that had accumulated and filling them with medicaments.

Abulcasis Abul Casem Chalaf Ben Abbas (1050-1122) was one of the greatest surgeons who recommended cauterization in cases of periapical abscess and fistula as well as described the process. His book contains one of the first representations of dental instruments. He also advocated ligation of teeth in cases of a blow or fall.

Thirteenth to Fifteenth Centuries

During 1130-1163 AD, a change of guard occurred when a series of Papal edicts prohibited monks from performing any type of surgery, bloodletting, or tooth extraction. After the edicts, barbers assumed the monks' surgical duties. In 1210 AD, a Guild of Barbers was established in France. Barbers eventually evolved into two groups: Surgeons who were educated and trained to perform complex surgical operations, and lay barbers or barber-surgeons, who performed more routine hygienic services including shaving, bleeding, and tooth extraction.

However, this was short-lived as in the 1400s - a series of royal decrees in France prohibited lay barbers from practicing all surgical procedures except bleeding, cupping, leeching, and extracting teeth. In 1530, the "Little Medicinal Book for All Kinds of Diseases and Infirmities of the Teeth" Artzney Buchlein, the first book devoted entirely to dentistry, was published in Germany.

THE GREEKS, ROMANS, AND ETRUSCANS

During the twelfth century BC, a Greek physician called Esculapius was recognized, according to Greek literature, as the inventor of purgatives and extraction of teeth. Therefore, the first documented origin of dental surgery can be thought to be from Esculapius. Celius Aurelianus mentioned a leaden instrument 'plumbeumodontagogoon' used for the extraction of teeth.

Greeks and Romans

The Greeks and Romans made significant contributions to early dentistry. Hippocrates, often regarded as the father of medicine, wrote extensively on dental problems and their treatments. He believed that oral health was essential for overall well-being. Physicians like Celsus recommended treatments for dental issues, and dental prosthetics, such as dentures, were developed during this time.

The Romans also made notable contributions to early dentistry. During the Roman Empire, oral health care was well-organized and more accessible. They had skilled dental practitioners who performed various dental procedures, from extractions to more complex surgical interventions.

One of the most remarkable achievements of Roman dentistry was the development of dental prosthetics, including dentures. These dentures were often crafted from materials like gold and silver, showcasing the Romans’ sophistication in dental care. While these prosthetics were limited to the wealthy and elite, their existence signaled an understanding of the importance of both functionality and aesthetics in oral health.

The Romans also created specialized dental instruments and tools, including scalers, forceps, and dental drills. These instruments paved the way for more precise dental procedures and improved patient comfort during treatment.

The period of 175 years from 1800 to 1975 represents significant advancement in prosthetic and restorative dental service. The transition from the time of ill-fitting dentures, fashioned from naturally occurring materials, to the application of synthetic resins for a long list of dental and surgical purposes, described in this symposium, represents a typical example of technical and professional advancement that has taken place throughout world society during this same period. If dentistry retains this good working relationship, as it is expected to do, then the advancements within the next two generations can significantly change and improve the practice of dentistry from what is presently known, by applying additional new and modified dental resins.

ANESTHESIA

The first-ever recorded use of any anesthetic agent dates back to 500 AD when Peruvians used coca leaves for psychotropic properties. However, it was not until 1540, when cordus synthesized ether, that any synthetic drug was used for anesthesia. Dr. Crawford W. Long in 1842 used ether as an anesthetic, and Dr. William Morton publicized ether in 1846. Cocaine was isolated, and hypodermic applications were made possible in 1859 by Dr. Karl Koller. The introduction of nitrous oxide, or laughing gas, into dental practice revolutionized pain management. It was first used by Horace Wells in the mid-19th century, laying the groundwork for modern anesthetics in dentistry. The development of anesthesia allowed for more complex and less painful dental procedures, contributing to the overall advancement of dental care.

INNOVATIONS IN DENTISTRY

The late 19th century and early 20th century saw numerous innovations in dental technology and techniques. The introduction of X-rays in 1895 by Wilhelm Conrad Roentgen provided dentists with a new tool for diagnosing dental issues. This technology allowed for the detection of cavities, root infections, and other hidden dental problems, greatly enhancing the accuracy of diagnoses.

The invention of the electric dental drill in the 1860s, attributed to George F. Green , marked a significant advancement in dental procedures. This new tool increased efficiency and precision in cavity preparations, leading to improved patient outcomes. The development of dental materials, such as amalgam and dental cements, further enhanced the practice, providing durable and reliable options for restoring teeth.

MODERN DENTISTRY

As the 20th century progressed, dentistry continued to evolve with advancements in materials, techniques, and technology. The introduction of fluoride in dental care significantly reduced the incidence of dental caries. Public health campaigns promoting oral hygiene, combined with the widespread use of fluoride toothpaste, contributed to better overall dental health.

The establishment of dental schools and professional organizations, such as the American Dental Association (ADA) and the British Dental Association (BDA), promoted the standardization of dental education and practices, leading to improved patient care.

In recent decades, the field of dentistry has witnessed remarkable advancements, including the development of digital imaging, laser dentistry, and minimally invasive techniques. The incorporation of technology has not only enhanced diagnostic capabilities but also improved treatment options and patient comfort.

THE FUTURE OF DENTISTRY

Looking ahead, the future of dentistry appears promising, with ongoing research and innovation paving the way for new treatment modalities and preventive strategies. Advances in biotechnology and regenerative medicine hold the potential to revolutionize dental practices, offering solutions for tooth regeneration and more effective treatments for periodontal disease.

The integration of artificial intelligence AI in dentistry is also gaining traction, with AI algorithms being developed to assist in diagnostics, treatment planning, and patient management. As technology continues to advance, the field of dentistry is poised for significant transformation, ultimately leading to better oral health outcomes for patients worldwide.

Great Dentists in History

Greene Vardiman Black

Greene Vardiman Black was one of the founders of modern dentistry in the United States. He is also known as the father of operative dentistry.

He researched many important topics to dentistry, including the cause of dental fluorosis and ideal cavity preparations. One of his many inventions was a foot-driven dental drill. He is also known for his principles of tooth preparations, in which he outlines the proper methods to prepare teeth for fillings. These cavity preparations used principles of engineering and material sciences to maximize strength and retention of the amalgam filling and minimize fractures, as well as tooth anatomy, to minimize pulp exposure.

In addition to developing a standard for cavity preparations, Black also experimented with various mixtures of amalgam. After years of experimentation, Black published his balanced amalgam formula in 1895. This formula and its variations quickly became the gold standard and would remain such for almost 70 years.

Pierre Fauchard

Pierre Fauchard was a French physician, credited as being the "father of modern dentistry." He is widely known for writing the first complete scientific description of dentistry, Le Chirurgien Dentiste ("The Surgeon Dentist"), published in 1728. The book described basic oral anatomy and function, signs and symptoms of oral pathology, operative methods for removing decay and restoring teeth, periodontal disease pyorrhea, orthodontics, replacement of missing teeth, and tooth transplantation.

Despite the limitations of the primitive surgical instruments during the late 17th and early 18th century, Fauchard was considered a highly skilled surgeon by many of his colleagues at Angers University Hospital. He made remarkable improvisations of dental instruments, often adapting tools from watchmakers, jewelers, and even barbers, that he thought could be used in dentistry.

Fauchard introduced dental fillings as treatment for dental cavities. He asserted that sugar derivative acids, like tartaric acid, were responsible for dental decay and also suggested that tumors surrounding the teeth, in the gums, could appear in the later stages of tooth decay.

Fauchard was the pioneer of dental prosthesis and discovered many methods to replace lost teeth. He suggested that substitutes could be made from carved blocks of ivory or bone and that these artificially-made dental pieces would be as useful as natural ones. One of his methods stated that the artificial teeth could be held in place by tying them to the remaining solid teeth by pivots, using waxed thread or gold wire.

Conclusion

the history of dentistry shows an amazing journey from early, simple treatments to today's advanced care. Over the years, dentistry has grown into a crucial part of healthcare, focused not only on treating problems but also on preventing them. As we move forward, dental care remains essential for maintaining overall health, ensuring that every smile continues to reflect the importance of taking care of our teeth.
